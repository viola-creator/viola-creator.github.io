Maana Kyoto

The original Maana home — a restored weaver's machiya where indigo-dyed fabrics once hung to dry in the courtyard.

Tucked within the Nishijin weaving quarter, this two-storey machiya was home to a textile artisan for over a century. The restoration preserved the original tsuchiura earthen walls, the timber frame, and the toori-niwa running kitchen — while adding underfloor heating, a modern kitchen, and a deep hinoki soaking bath overlooking the garden.

The ground floor opens onto a small tsuboniwa courtyard with moss and a single maple. Upstairs, two bedrooms face the rooftops of the neighbourhood. Morning light enters through shoji screens; evenings are best spent in the bath, watching the garden fade to shadow.

Set along the western bank of the Kamo River in the Shimogamo neighbourhood, this machiya was originally a merchant's residence. Its long, narrow footprint — the "eel's bed" form typical of Kyoto — runs from street to garden to river, catching breezes through the entire house.

Three tatami bedrooms open onto a shared engawa overlooking the courtyard. The ground floor has a formal sitting room with tokonoma alcove, a modern kitchen, and a dining space that seats eight. In spring, cherry petals drift into the courtyard; in autumn, the maple turns the garden red.

Three machiya share a stone-paved roji alley on a quiet slope below Kiyomizu-dera. Originally the homes of pottery families, each has been restored as a self-contained residence — with its own entrance, kitchen, bath, and garden — while an interior passage allows groups to connect all three into a single, sprawling home.

Choose the unit that suits your group, or book the entire compound for gatherings of up to ten.

New Residence · Opening Soon

Maana Annex

A purpose-built house adjacent to Maana Kyoto. Contemporary architecture grounded in Kyoto's material traditions — stone, timber, plaster, light.

← Stay

Designed as a companion to the original Maana Kyoto machiya, this new-build residence sits steps from its predecessor yet inhabits a different architectural language. Where the machiya speaks of restoration and memory, the Annex speaks of intention and craft — walls of polished plaster, floors of reclaimed timber, windows that frame the garden like hanging scrolls.

The house offers a single booking: yours alone. Three bedrooms, a private garden, and the quiet luxury of contemporary space built with traditional methods. Every material was chosen in Kyoto; every joint was made by hand.

Morning Tea Ceremony and Breakfast Workshop 朝茶事

Arrive before the city wakes. In the quiet of the tearoom, your host will prepare matcha using seasonal utensils. The ceremony is followed by a traditional Kyoto-style breakfast — rice, miso, pickles, grilled fish, and seasonal small plates — served in the adjacent dining room.
